Ethical considerations

This map publishes only aggregated data. It exists alongside a statement of serious concerns about the underlying personal data and how it has been handled.

Report to the ICO

I have treated this matter as a personal data breach under the UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) and have reported it to the Information Commissioner’s Office (ICO). I have grave concerns about the handling of these datasets — including how they were compiled, shared, and what further processing may be contemplated.

Data protection roles and processing scope

I was engaged by a third party who, so far as I can determine, retains the original datasets and appears to act as data controller in respect of the personal data they contain (postal addresses relating to identifiable data subjects, associated comment text, and timestamps). I was instructed to act as data processor: to carry out a defined operation of processing on those datasets on the controller’s behalf — data extraction from more than one hundred planning-feedback forms that were not designated for the profiling of local Scientologists — not to determine the purposes of processing myself.

That instruction appeared limited and proportionate at the outset: structured extraction from planning consultation responses, not profiling. It did not, on its face, entail publication of location data or identification of individuals. After processing was completed, the controller indicated an intention to consider further use of address data — a purpose and scope beyond what I had been engaged to perform. That raises serious questions under the principles of purpose limitation (Article 5(1)(b) UK GDPR) and lawfulness, fairness, and transparency (Article 5(1)(a)), and as to whether an appropriate lawful basis under Article 6 exists for any wider processing.

I did not solicit these datasets, was not party to their original collection, and have not agreed to any processing beyond the initial brief. I have seen no adequate Article 28 processor terms (or equivalent safeguards) addressing confidentiality, security, sub-processing, or deletion. I have no confidence in the controller’s compliance with the obligations of a data controller, including confidentiality and security. By way of example only: within the past fortnight, a mutual contact reported that the controller had revealed to them the identity of another person that contact had supplied in confidence — which reinforces my concern as to integrity and confidentiality of processing.

Re-identification risk

Even where extracted comment text appears supportive or favourable, the combination of comments with precise address data materially increases the risk that data subjects can be identified, particularly in close-knit communities or where addresses are distinctive. That is why raw datasets, addresses, and comment text are not published on this site, and why only aggregated outputs — with minimum cell thresholds and spatial jitter — are released.

Attempts to raise concerns

I have sought to raise these matters with organisations that represent themselves as acting in the interests of current and former cult-members. I have not received a substantive response. A meeting was scheduled and subsequently cancelled without explanation. I was later invited to submit material on condition that it would be relayed directly to the data controller — a course I regard as inappropriate where data subjects’ privacy may already be compromised and where I have no confidence in that party’s handling of personal data.

Purpose of this publication

My concern is to protect the privacy of data subjects who may be affected, not to facilitate identification or targeting. Organisations purporting to safeguard vulnerable persons should not engage in profiling or related processing inconsistent with that aim, and should not route concerns back to a controller in whom I have no confidence.

This statement records the site operator’s understanding and concerns. It does not constitute legal advice. Data subjects who believe their personal data have been misused may wish to contact the ICO or obtain independent legal advice.

Privacy & how this map works

What this map shows

Areas where the source dataset has multiple geocoded entries with comments treated as positive / supportive for publication only. Colour indicates relative density within published hexagons. It is meant to confirm broad geographic patterns for people who already know the underlying data — not to locate individual homes.

What is stored on this website

This site serves only static files. There is no database and no search API.

  • Published: hexagon outlines, grouped counts per hex, and density bands (ranges, not exact numbers in the UI).
  • Not published: original addresses, postcodes, comments, timestamps, CSV rows, or coordinates of individual properties.

What happens before anything is published

All of the following runs offline on the operator’s machine. Only the aggregated output is copied to this site:

  1. Publication filter — for this map only, rows whose comments are not classified as positive/supporting are dropped; their addresses are never geocoded for deployment.
  2. Geocoding — addresses are converted to coordinates locally; results are cached on disk and are not deployed.
  3. Jitter — each point is shifted by a random offset (about 130m) before grouping, so pins are not placed on exact building locations.
  4. Hexagonal aggregation — points are combined into ~460 m H3 hexagons (resolution 8).
  5. k-anonymity — hexagons with fewer than 4 contributing records are discarded entirely.
  6. Strip & audit — deploy bundles are checked to ensure they do not contain addresses, postcodes, or per-property coordinates.

Preventing reverse engineering of addresses

  • No point layer — you cannot click a house; only large hex areas are shown.
  • Minimum group size (k) — isolated or rare addresses never appear as a lone cell on the map.
  • Coarse cells + jitter — a hex covers many buildings; jitter prevents alignment with a single doorway.
  • Zoom limit — the map cannot be zoomed in to building level (max zoom 13).
  • Banded counts on hover — e.g. “5–9”, not “7 at this address”.
  • No address search — there is no geocoder or lookup endpoint on this site.

Someone who already possesses the full address list could still compare it to this map. This design prevents new identification by visitors who only see the published aggregates.

What the colour scale means

Colours are relative within the published hexagons on this map — not absolute population counts for the town.

  • Lighter amber — lower density band among shown cells.
  • Orange / red — higher density band among shown cells.

Hover text matches colour: each distinct total in a hex gets its own shade (e.g. “7 addresses in area”, “10–11 addresses in area”). That is still a group count, not an address list.

Verify this deployment

All map logic and privacy checks are in the public repository cultpodcasts/apostate-leaks. This site records the exact Git commit it was built from (see the bar at the top). Compare that commit to the published published map files (R2, not in public git) and run npm run audit in offline-pipeline locally to confirm no addresses are shipped.

Limitations

Hexagons can still reveal that “several supportive responses came from this general neighbourhood”. In very sparse areas, even grouped data may feel sensitive — that is why small groups are suppressed.
